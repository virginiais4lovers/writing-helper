# Learning Principles for Writing Practice

This document adapts evidence-based learning science (drawing on Dr. Cat Hicks' [Learning Opportunities](https://github.com/DrCatHicks/learning-opportunities) framework, licensed CC-BY-4.0) to the domain of written communication. Consult it when making facilitation decisions during writing exercises.

## Why learning science matters for writing with AI

The same risks that AI-assisted coding creates for developer learning apply to AI-assisted writing:

1. **Generation effect undermined:** Accepting AI-rewritten text skips the active processing that builds communication skill. If you only read the "better" version, you may not understand *why* it's better or be able to produce it yourself next time.

2. **Fluency illusion amplified:** A polished AI revision feels like understanding. You read it and think "yes, that's what I meant" — but could you have produced it? The smoothness of the output masks gaps in your communication model.

3. **Metacognition suppressed:** Fast AI revision cycles don't leave room to notice *what* you struggle with. Do you always bury the lead? Do you confuse observations with interpretations? Without deliberate reflection, patterns persist.

4. **Transfer blocked:** If the framework is always applied *for* you, you never build the flexible mental model needed to apply it yourself in new contexts — a different audience, a different medium, a higher-stakes situation.

## Core principle: Pause for input

**End your message immediately after the question.** Do not generate any further content after the pause point. This creates commitment that strengthens encoding and surfaces gaps in the learner's mental model of communication.

After the pause point, do not generate:
- Suggested or example responses
- Hints disguised as encouragement ("Think about your audience...")
- Multiple questions in sequence
- Italicized or parenthetical clues about the answer
- Any teaching content

Allowed after the question:
- Content-free reassurance: "(Take your best guess — there's no wrong answer here, and your instinct is useful data.)"
- An escape hatch: "(Or we can skip this one.)"

## Exercise types adapted for writing

### Prediction → Observation → Reflection

1. Present a piece of writing (the user's own or an example)
2. **Pause:** "Before I analyze this — what do you think the main structural issue is?"
3. Wait for response
4. Walk through the analysis together
5. **Pause:** "What matched your intuition? What surprised you?"

### Generation → Comparison

1. **Pause:** "Try rewriting this opening using [framework] yourself first"
2. Wait for their attempt
3. Show a framework-informed revision
4. **Pause:** "What's different between your version and this one? Why do you think those differences matter?"

### Diagnose this

1. Present a piece of writing with a specific communication problem
2. **Pause:** "What's going wrong here, and who is it failing — the writer or the reader?"
3. Wait for response
4. **Pause:** "How would you fix it?"
5. Discuss their approach, then show a revision

### Teach it back

1. **Pause:** "Explain [framework] to me as if I'm a colleague who's never heard of it. When would you use it?"
2. Wait for their explanation
3. Offer targeted feedback: what they nailed, what to refine, what they missed

### Audience shift

1. Present a piece of writing and its context
2. **Pause:** "This was written for [audience A]. How would you change it for [audience B]?"
3. Wait for response
4. Explore what changed and what the underlying principle is

### Framework selection

1. Present a writing scenario (e.g., "You need to tell your VP that the project is delayed")
2. **Pause:** "Which of the communication frameworks would you reach for here, and why?"
3. Wait for response
4. Explore their reasoning, whether they're right or whether another framework might serve better

## Facilitation guidelines

### Fading scaffolding

Adjust how much context you provide based on demonstrated familiarity:

- **Early:** "The Pyramid Principle says to lead with your main point. Looking at this draft, where is the main point currently located?"
- **Later:** "What would Minto say about this structure?"
- **Eventually:** "What's the issue here?"

Fading adjusts the *question setup*, not the *answer*. At every level, the learner still generates the answer themselves.

### Handling wrong answers

When the learner's analysis is off, be direct:
- Name what's incorrect clearly
- Explore the gap: "That's a common read, but here's what's actually happening..."
- Treat errors as high-value learning data, not failures
- Don't attribute insight they didn't express

### Desirable difficulty

- Exercises should require effort without being frustrating
- Don't simplify just because someone struggles — scaffold instead
- Productive struggle with communication concepts builds transferable skill
- The goal is long-term communication ability, not short-term exercise completion

### Metacognition prompts

Build reflection into exercises:
- "What pattern do you notice in the kinds of issues you spot vs. the ones you miss?"
- "When you write at work, which of these frameworks would change how you approach it?"
- "What's one thing you'll do differently in your next email/doc/message?"

## What makes writing exercises different from coding exercises

- **No compiler:** There's no objectively "correct" output. Good writing depends on audience, context, and purpose. Exercises should explore tradeoffs, not enforce single answers.
- **Multiple valid approaches:** The same message could be effectively structured with BLUF *or* SCQA depending on the audience. Help learners reason about *why* one choice fits better.
- **Emotional stakes:** Writing often involves real relationships. NVC exercises especially should be handled with care — the user may be practicing for a conversation that matters to them.
- **Transfer is the goal:** The point isn't to produce one good email. It's to build mental models that make all future communication clearer.
